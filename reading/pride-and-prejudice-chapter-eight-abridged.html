Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Eight (Abridged)

By the time Elizabeth came down to dinner at Netherfield, Jane was still ill. Everyone politely asked about her health, but Mr. Bingley’s concern was clearly more sincere than anyone else’s. His sisters said all the right things and repeated how unpleasant illness was, yet they did not seem deeply troubled. Mr. Bingley, however, showed real anxiety for Jane, and his kindness toward Elizabeth was warm and natural. Elizabeth was grateful for his attention and could see how much he cared about her sister.

The rest of the party did not impress her. Miss Bingley spent most of her time trying to attract Mr. Darcy’s notice, and Mrs. Hurst followed her example. Mr. Hurst appeared lazy and uninterested in everything except eating, drinking, and cards. After dinner, Elizabeth returned to Jane’s room. As soon as she left, Miss Bingley began criticizing her. She declared that Elizabeth’s manners were poor and that she had no style, no conversation, and no beauty. Darcy did not support this cruel judgment completely. Though he said little, he remarked that Elizabeth’s eyes had a fine expression, which was more praise than Miss Bingley expected.

Elizabeth continued to spend most of her time caring for Jane. On the following day, Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst invited her to sit with them in the drawing room. She agreed, taking some work with her, while Darcy wrote a letter. His neat handwriting and calm concentration attracted attention, and Miss Bingley tried repeatedly to flatter him. She praised his writing, offered to mend his pen, and hinted that his sister Georgiana would be delighted to receive such a letter. Darcy answered politely, but without much interest. Elizabeth watched this with quiet amusement.

When Miss Bingley turned the conversation to Darcy’s sister, she praised Georgiana’s beauty, elegance, and accomplishments. Darcy spoke of his sister with genuine affection, and Elizabeth noticed that beneath his proud manner, he was capable of strong family feeling. Soon the talk moved to the idea of an “accomplished woman.” Miss Bingley listed the usual fashionable skills: music, drawing, dancing, singing, and modern languages. Darcy agreed, but he added something more demanding. In his opinion, a truly accomplished woman must also improve her mind by extensive reading. Elizabeth laughed at this and said she was no longer surprised at his knowing only six accomplished women. She was surprised he knew that many.

This playful answer amused Darcy, though Miss Bingley did not enjoy it. Elizabeth’s intelligence and liveliness continued to interest him. Later, while Jane was resting, Elizabeth joined the others again. Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst walked around the room with Darcy, hoping he would admire them. Elizabeth was invited to join, but she refused, guessing that they wanted either to display their figures or to have the pleasure of criticizing her if she walked with them. Darcy asked which of these motives she meant, and Elizabeth replied with humor that perhaps both were true. Her wit made the exchange lively, and Darcy listened with growing interest.

That evening, when the conversation turned to character and faults, Darcy admitted that he found it difficult to forget the follies and weaknesses of others once they had offended him. Elizabeth challenged him lightly, suggesting that such resentment was itself a weakness. Their conversation revealed an important contrast: Darcy valued seriousness, control, and good judgment, while Elizabeth valued laughter, flexibility, and human warmth. Yet instead of separating them, these differences seemed to draw Darcy’s attention more strongly toward her. Although Elizabeth still considered him proud and often disagreeable, she could not deny that his mind was worth engaging. At Netherfield, while Jane slowly recovered, the connection between Elizabeth and Darcy quietly deepened through conversation, teasing, and mutual observation.

Vocabulary List

  • sincere — honest and genuine.
  • anxiety — worry or concern.
  • criticizing — saying negative things about someone.
  • judgment — an opinion formed after thinking carefully.
  • concentration — careful attention.
  • flatter — to praise someone too much to gain favor.
  • accomplished — highly skilled and educated.
  • extensive — large in amount.
  • resentment — lasting anger about something unfair.
  • flexibility — willingness to adapt or change.
  • engaging — interesting and pleasant.
  • observation — careful watching or noticing.
  • elegance — graceful beauty and style.
  • genuine — real and true.
  • liveliness — energy and spirited charm.
