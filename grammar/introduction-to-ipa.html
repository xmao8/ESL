Introduction to the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)

The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) is a standardized system of symbols—based on Latin and Greek letters—designed to represent every sound in human speech. Unlike English spelling, where one letter can have many sounds and one sound many spellings, each IPA symbol stands for exactly one sound.

Why the IPA Is Important

  • Accurate Pronunciation: When you see /kæt/, you know exactly how to say “cat,” no matter your native language.
  • Overcoming Spelling Confusion: A single symbol (e.g. /ʃ/) covers the “sh” sound in shoe, sugar, mission, special.
  • Learning New Languages: The IPA teaches you unfamiliar sounds from the start, so you can pronounce words correctly even if they don’t exist in your mother tongue.
  • Speech Therapy & Linguistics: Professionals use IPA to diagnose speech impediments and to compare sounds across languages.
  • Standardization: A linguist in Tokyo and one in São Paulo read the same symbols and understand the same sounds.

How the IPA Works

The IPA chart organizes sounds by how and where they’re made in the mouth and throat.

Consonants

  • /p/ as in pat (lips closed)
  • /tʃ/ as in church (t + “sh”)
  • /ð/ as in this (voiced “th”)
  • /s/ as in sun (air through narrow opening)
  • /ŋ/ as in sing (velar nasal)
  • /w/ as in we (labial-velar approximant)

Vowels

Vowels are labeled by tongue height (high/low), tongue position (front/back), and lip rounding.

  • /iː/ as in sheep (high front, unrounded)
  • /æ/ as in cat (low front, unrounded)
  • /uː/ as in moon (high back, rounded)
  • /ɑː/ as in car (low back, unrounded)
  • /e/ as in bed (mid front, unrounded)
  • /ə/ as in about (central, unstressed schwa)

Diphthongs

Diphthongs glide from one vowel position to another within the same syllable.

  • /aɪ/ as in my (from /a/ to /ɪ/)
  • /eɪ/ as in face (from /e/ to /ɪ/)
  • /oʊ/ as in boat (from /o/ to /ʊ/)
  • /aʊ/ as in cow (from /a/ to /ʊ/)
  • /ɔɪ/ as in boy (from /ɔ/ to /ɪ/)

Reading IPA in Dictionaries

Pronunciations are often shown in slashes /…/ for broad transcription. You may also see square brackets […] for narrow transcription with extra detail.

  • information → /ˌɪnfərˈmeɪʃən/
  • television → /ˈtelɪvɪʒən/
  • beautiful → /ˈbjuːtɪfl̩/
  • through → /θruː/
  • knowledge → /ˈnɒlɪdʒ/
