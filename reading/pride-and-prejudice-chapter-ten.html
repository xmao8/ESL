Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Ten

The day passed quietly at Netherfield. Elizabeth spent most of her time with Jane, whose illness continued, though she was slowly improving. When Elizabeth joined the others, she found them engaged in polite but somewhat artificial conversation.

Miss Bingley made several attempts to draw Mr. Darcy into conversation, often speaking about his sister and her accomplishments. She praised his sister’s beauty and talents, hoping to engage his interest. Darcy responded politely but without much enthusiasm.

Elizabeth, meanwhile, sat sewing and listening. Miss Bingley soon invited her to walk about the room, suggesting that such movement might improve her spirits. Elizabeth agreed, and the two women began walking together.

Miss Bingley then invited Mr. Darcy to join them. He declined, saying he could imagine only two reasons for their walking: either they wished to discuss private matters, or they were displaying their figures. In either case, he preferred to remain seated.

Elizabeth laughed at his remark, while Miss Bingley pretended to be offended. She continued to try to engage him, asking what could be done to punish such behavior. Elizabeth suggested that teasing him might be the best method.

Darcy replied that he was not easily teased, as he tried to avoid weaknesses that could be laughed at. Elizabeth answered that everyone has faults, and that pride is one of his. Darcy admitted that pride might indeed be a weakness, though he distinguished it from vanity. Pride, he said, relates to our opinion of ourselves, while vanity concerns what others think of us.

The conversation continued with wit and playfulness. Elizabeth enjoyed challenging Darcy’s ideas, while he found himself increasingly interested in her intelligence and spirit. Though he tried to maintain his usual reserve, he could not entirely hide his admiration.

Miss Bingley, noticing this, became more determined to attract Darcy’s attention. She spoke again of accomplishments, elegance, and fashionable society, but her efforts were less successful than she hoped.

Later, the group turned to reading. Miss Bingley selected a book but soon lost interest, complaining that she could not focus. She attempted once more to engage Darcy, but he remained calm and distant.

Elizabeth, on the other hand, showed quiet independence. She neither tried to impress Darcy nor sought his approval. This natural behavior made her more appealing to him, even as he struggled against his growing feelings.

The chapter highlights the developing tension between Darcy’s pride and his attraction to Elizabeth. It also contrasts Elizabeth’s genuine character with Miss Bingley’s artificial attempts to gain attention.

Vocabulary

  • accomplishment — a learned skill
  • reserve — calm and controlled behavior
  • tease — to joke about someone
  • vanity — concern about others’ opinions
  • independence — not needing approval from others
  • admiration — strong liking or respect
