Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Seven (Abridged)

The Bennet family’s property brought in about two thousand a year, but it was entailed on a distant male relative. This meant that when Mr. Bennet died, his daughters would not inherit the estate. Mrs. Bennet had some money of her own, but it was not enough to fully support the family. She also had relatives nearby, including her sister Mrs. Phillips in Meryton, whom the younger girls visited often.

Meryton was only a mile from Longbourn, and Lydia and Kitty especially enjoyed going there several times a week. Their main reason for visiting was to see the officers of a militia regiment that had recently arrived in the town. The regiment would remain for the winter, and this brought great excitement. The girls learned the officers’ names and soon met them. Their uncle introduced them, and they talked of nothing but officers.

Mr. Bennet listened and said they were two of the silliest girls in the country. Kitty was embarrassed, but Lydia continued talking happily. Mrs. Bennet defended them, saying young girls naturally enjoy such things. She even admitted she still liked officers herself and would gladly accept a rich one as a husband for her daughters.

At that moment, a letter arrived for Jane from Miss Bingley, inviting her to dine at Netherfield. Mrs. Bennet was delighted and encouraged her to go on horseback, hoping it would rain so Jane would have to stay overnight. Soon after Jane left, it began to rain heavily.

The next morning, Jane wrote that she was unwell after getting wet. She had a sore throat and headache and would remain at Netherfield. Mrs. Bennet was pleased, but Elizabeth was worried. She decided to walk there to see her sister.

Elizabeth set out alone and walked quickly across fields and over puddles. By the time she arrived, she was tired, dirty, and out of breath—but determined to take care of Jane.

Vocabulary List

  • entailed — legally passed to a specific heir.
  • militia — a group of local soldiers.
  • admiration — strong liking or respect.
  • sensible — reasonable and practical.
  • invited — asked to come somewhere.
  • encouraged — gave support or advice.
  • heavily — strongly or with force.
  • unwell — not feeling healthy.
  • worried — feeling concern.
  • determined — decided firmly.
