Using Could & Might

What Are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs (like could and might) are auxiliary verbs that express ability, permission, possibility, or advice. They do not change form for different subjects and are followed by the base form of the main verb.

1. Using Could

Could has several functions:

  • Past ability: Express what someone was able to do in the past. Example: When I was younger, I could run five miles.
  • Polite permission/request: Ask or grant permission in a polite way. Example: Could I borrow your pen?
  • Possibility: Suggest something is possible now or in the future (less certain than “can”). Example: It could rain this afternoon.
  • Suggestion: Offer a possible course of action. Example: You could try restarting your computer.

2. Using Might

Might usually expresses a lower degree of certainty than “might” or “could” in some contexts:

  • Possibility: Indicate something is possible but uncertain. Example: She might join us later.
  • Polite suggestion: Offer advice tentatively. Example: You might check the schedule again.
  • Past possibility (with have + past participle): Speculate about something that could have happened. Example: They might have missed the train.

3. Could vs. Might

Both express possibility, but might often suggests a weaker possibility than could in present/future contexts.

Function Could Might
Present/Future possibility It could snow tomorrow. It might snow tomorrow.
Past ability/possibility He could speak French at age five. He might have seen the movie already.
Politeness Could you help me? You might consider calling her.

4. Common Mistakes

  • Avoid using could or might with “to” before the main verb. Incorrect: She could to finish on time. Correct: She could finish on time.
  • Don’t use could or might in the past simple form; they stay the same for all subjects and tenses.
