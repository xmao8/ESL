Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Ten (Abridged)

The day at Netherfield passed quietly, with Elizabeth spending most of her time caring for her sister Jane, who was still recovering from her illness. Whenever Elizabeth joined the others, she found herself observing their behavior rather than fully participating in their conversations. She noticed that Mr. Bingley remained cheerful and attentive, especially toward Jane, while his sisters were more concerned with appearances and social refinement.

Miss Bingley made several attempts to engage Mr. Darcy in conversation, often speaking about his sister and her many accomplishments. She praised her beauty, her talents, and her elegant manners, hoping to interest Darcy and draw him into discussion. Darcy responded politely, but without much enthusiasm, showing little desire to continue the topic.

Elizabeth, seated quietly with her work, listened with amusement. Miss Bingley soon invited her to walk about the room, suggesting that the movement would improve her spirits. Elizabeth accepted, and the two women began walking together. Miss Bingley then asked Mr. Darcy to join them, but he refused. He explained that he could imagine only two reasons for their walking: either they wished to discuss private matters, or they were showing off their figures. In either case, he preferred to remain seated.

This remark made Elizabeth laugh, while Miss Bingley pretended to be offended. She asked what could be done to punish such behavior, and Elizabeth suggested that teasing him might be the best method. Darcy replied that he was not easily teased because he tried to avoid weaknesses that could be laughed at.

Elizabeth answered that everyone has faults, and she accused Darcy of pride. He admitted that pride might indeed be a weakness but made a distinction between pride and vanity. Pride, he explained, relates to how we think of ourselves, while vanity concerns what we want others to think of us. Elizabeth found this conversation both entertaining and revealing, as it showed Darcy’s serious and thoughtful nature.

As the discussion continued, Elizabeth challenged Darcy’s ideas with wit and confidence. He found himself increasingly interested in her intelligence and lively manner of speaking. Although he tried to remain reserved, he could not entirely hide his growing admiration. Elizabeth, however, remained unaware of this change in his feelings. To her, he was still the proud man who had once dismissed her as only “tolerable.”

Miss Bingley began to notice Darcy’s attention toward Elizabeth and became more determined to win his interest. She returned to the subject of accomplishments, describing the qualities that a truly accomplished woman should possess. According to her, such a woman must be skilled in music, drawing, dancing, and languages, as well as graceful and elegant in her manners. Darcy agreed but added that she must also improve her mind through extensive reading.

Elizabeth listened and expressed her doubt that many women could meet such high expectations. She said she had never seen so many accomplished women as they described. This response amused Darcy but slightly annoyed Miss Bingley, who believed Elizabeth was undervaluing the importance of refinement and education.

Later, the group turned to reading. Miss Bingley selected a book but quickly grew bored and abandoned it. She continued to seek Darcy’s attention through conversation, but he remained distant. Elizabeth, on the other hand, showed independence and calm confidence. She did not attempt to impress anyone, and this natural behavior made her even more attractive to Darcy.

Throughout the evening, the contrast between the characters became clearer. Elizabeth’s sincerity and intelligence stood out against Miss Bingley’s artificial manners and social ambition. Darcy’s struggle between his pride and his growing admiration for Elizabeth became more noticeable, even though he tried to conceal it.

This chapter deepens the development of their relationship, showing how Elizabeth’s natural charm begins to challenge Darcy’s prejudices, while also revealing the influence of pride, social expectations, and personal character.

Vocabulary

  • refinement — elegance and good manners
  • enthusiasm — strong interest or excitement
  • vanity — concern about others’ opinions
  • independence — not needing approval from others
  • confidence — belief in oneself
  • admiration — strong liking or respect
