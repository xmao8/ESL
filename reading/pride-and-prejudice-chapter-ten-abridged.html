Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Ten (Abridged)

The day passed much as the day before had done. Jane continued to mend, though slowly, and Elizabeth spent part of the morning with her. In the evening she joined the party in the drawing-room, where Mr. Darcy was engaged in writing a letter, and Miss Bingley, seated near him, watched his progress with eager admiration.

She frequently interrupted him with messages to his sister and praises of his handwriting, but Darcy bore her attentions with calm politeness. Mr. Bingley, meanwhile, amused himself at cards with Mr. Hurst, and Elizabeth observed the scene with quiet amusement.

The conversation soon turned to writing. Miss Bingley admired Darcy’s long letters, while Bingley confessed that he wrote carelessly, his ideas flowing too quickly for proper expression. Elizabeth replied with playful irony that such humility must protect him from criticism. Darcy, however, remarked that apparent modesty is often only a form of pride, and that people frequently value themselves for faults which they believe to arise from superior qualities.

He went on to observe that quickness of action, though admired, might lead to careless decisions. Bingley laughed at this and declared that he disliked arguments, preferring ease and cheerfulness. Elizabeth agreed, and Darcy soon returned to finish his letter.

When the writing was completed, Miss Bingley seated herself at the pianoforte and began to play. Elizabeth listened, turning over the music, and could not help noticing that Mr. Darcy’s eyes were often fixed upon her. She hardly knew how to interpret such attention, and concluded that he observed her only to find fault; yet the idea did not trouble her, for she liked him too little to care for his opinion.

After some music, Darcy approached Elizabeth and asked whether she felt inclined to dance a reel. She smiled, but refused, saying that she suspected he wished her to agree only that he might despise her taste. She preferred, she added, to disappoint him and avoid his judgment altogether. Darcy replied with courtesy that he did not dare despise her.

Elizabeth, who had expected to provoke him, was surprised by his civility. There was in her manner a mixture of arch playfulness and sweetness which made it difficult to offend anyone; and Darcy found himself increasingly charmed by her. In spite of his former objections to her connections, he could not deny the attraction of her lively mind and engaging manner.

Miss Bingley, observing this growing admiration, felt a rising jealousy. Her concern for Jane’s recovery was not entirely disinterested, for she wished to see Elizabeth removed from Netherfield as soon as possible. Thus, while Jane improved, the quiet interactions of the household revealed changing feelings, particularly in Darcy, whose interest in Elizabeth was daily increasing.

Vocabulary List

  • admiration — strong approval or liking.
  • politeness — good manners.
  • carelessly — without attention.
  • modesty — not thinking too highly of oneself.
  • irony — saying the opposite of what is meant.
  • engaging — charming and interesting.
  • civility — polite behavior.
  • inclined — likely or willing.
  • jealousy — feeling of envy.
  • attraction — interest or liking.
