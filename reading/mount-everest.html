Local communities, including the Sherpa people, have lived in the Himalayan valleys for centuries. Sherpas are famous for their strength, skill, and knowledge of the mountains. Many serve as guides and porters, helping climbers reach the summit. For them, Everest is not only a mountain but also a sacred place connected to their traditions.

The first successful climb to the top was achieved in 1953 by Sir Edmund Hillary of New Zealand and Tenzing Norgay, a Sherpa climber from Nepal. Their achievement became a symbol of human determination and cooperation. Since then, thousands of climbers from around the world have attempted the dangerous journey, though not all succeed. Avalanches, thin air, freezing temperatures, and sudden storms make Everest one of the most dangerous places on Earth.

Despite the risks, climbing Everest remains a dream for many adventurers. Each year, hundreds of climbers set out during the spring season when weather is most stable. They must pass through several camps and spend weeks adjusting to the thin air at high altitudes. Everest also faces challenges from human activity. Increasing numbers of climbers have left behind trash, damaged trails, and created overcrowding near the summit. Climate change is melting glaciers and making avalanches more frequent. Efforts are now being made to protect the mountain, including clean-up campaigns and limits on the number of climbing permits.

Mount Everest is more than a peak. It is a symbol of human courage, the beauty of nature, and the importance of respect for our planet. For climbers, standing on its summit means touching the sky.
