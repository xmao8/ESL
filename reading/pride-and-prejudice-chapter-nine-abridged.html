Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Nine (Abridged)

Elizabeth spent the night at Netherfield taking care of her sister Jane, who was still unwell. In the morning, she was able to give Mr. Bingley a somewhat reassuring report about Jane’s condition. Although she joined the others at breakfast, she did not feel particularly welcome. Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst received her with cool politeness, and their lack of warmth reminded Elizabeth of her earlier dislike of them.

Mr. Bingley, however, behaved very differently. He showed genuine concern for Jane and treated Elizabeth with kindness and attention. His friendly manner helped Elizabeth feel less uncomfortable, even though she believed that the others considered her presence unnecessary.

After breakfast, Elizabeth observed the behavior of the Netherfield party more closely. Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst appeared polite when they chose to be, but Elizabeth saw clearly that they were proud and often looked down on others. Mr. Hurst showed little interest in conversation, caring mainly about eating and playing cards. In contrast, Mr. Bingley remained cheerful and attentive, especially toward Jane.

Later in the morning, Mrs. Bennet arrived at Netherfield with Lydia and Kitty. She had hoped to find Jane recovered, but was disappointed to learn that her daughter was still in bed. However, Mrs. Bennet was pleased to see how much attention Mr. Bingley gave to Jane, and she expressed her satisfaction openly. She spoke warmly about Jane’s beauty and the kindness of the Netherfield family, clearly enjoying the situation.

Miss Bingley received Mrs. Bennet politely but without real warmth. Soon, she began to speak about the advantages of city life, praising the elegance of London and suggesting that the country was less refined. Mrs. Bennet, who did not fully understand all her remarks, nevertheless defended her home and insisted that she preferred the country.

The conversation then turned to the Bennet daughters. Mrs. Bennet spoke freely and with little restraint, praising her girls and especially Jane’s beauty. Elizabeth listened with increasing embarrassment, aware that her mother’s behavior was too open and lacked proper form. She noticed that Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst exchanged amused glances, clearly finding Mrs. Bennet’s manner improper.

Mr. Darcy, who observed the scene quietly, was not impressed. He watched Mrs. Bennet’s behavior with a critical eye, and his opinion of the Bennet family did not improve. Elizabeth felt this deeply and was ashamed of her mother’s lack of discretion.

Mr. Bingley, however, seemed unaware of anything awkward. His attention remained focused on Jane, and he continued to show genuine interest in her comfort and recovery. His good nature prevented him from noticing any faults in the Bennet family.

The visit did not last long. Mrs. Bennet, satisfied with what she had seen, soon prepared to leave, taking Lydia and Kitty with her. Elizabeth remained at Netherfield to continue caring for Jane.

After they left, Miss Bingley spoke openly about the Bennet family, criticizing their manners and behavior. She particularly disapproved of the younger sisters, whom she considered foolish. Mr. Darcy agreed to some extent, though he admitted that Elizabeth was different and possessed qualities that set her apart from the rest of her family.

This chapter highlights the contrast between characters. Mr. Bingley’s kindness and sincerity stand out against his sisters’ pride and judgment. Mrs. Bennet’s lack of self-control causes embarrassment, while Elizabeth shows growing awareness of her family’s faults. At the same time, Darcy’s observations begin to reveal his complicated feelings toward Elizabeth, as he recognizes her intelligence despite his criticism of her family.

Vocabulary

  • reassuring — making someone feel less worried
  • presence — being in a place
  • refined — elegant and cultured
  • discretion — careful and appropriate behavior
  • critical — judging or finding faults
  • sincerity — honesty and truthfulness
