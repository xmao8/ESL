Possessive Adjectives vs. Possessive Pronouns

 
   

Possessive adjectives and possessive pronouns are both used to indicate ownership or possession, but they play distinct roles in sentence structure. Understanding their differences is essential for accurate English grammar.

   

Possessive Adjectives

   
     
Definition:
     
Words that modify a noun to show ownership or relationship. They always appear before the noun they describe.
     
What they answer:
     
Typically answer questions like: Whose?
     
List of Possessive Adjectives:
     
       
             
  • my (e.g., my car)
    •          
  • your (e.g., your book)
    •          
  • his (e.g., his dog)
    •          
  • her (e.g., her idea)
    •          
  • its (e.g., its tail)
    •          
  • our (e.g., our house)
    •          
  • their (e.g., their parents)
    •          
  • whose (e.g., Whose bag is this?)
    •        
     
     
Examples:
     
       
             
  • This is my phone. (describes the noun "phone")
    •          
  • Is that your umbrella? (describes the noun "umbrella")
    •          
  • The dog wagged its tail happily. (describes the noun "tail")
    •        
     
   
   

Possessive Pronouns

   
     
Definition:
     
Words that replace a noun or noun phrase to show ownership. They stand alone and do not precede a noun.
     
What they answer:
     
Typically answer questions like: Whose?
     
List of Possessive Pronouns:
     
       
             
  • mine (e.g., That car is mine.)
    •          
  • yours (e.g., Is this book yours?)
    •          
  • his (e.g., The blue one is his.)
    •          
  • hers (e.g., That coat is hers.)
    •          
  • ours (e.g., The victory was ours.)
    •          
  • theirs (e.g., The decision is theirs.)
    •          
  • whose (e.g., Whose is this?)
    •        
     
     
Note:
     
The word "its" is generally not used as a possessive pronoun, though it can be seen in some very specific or idiomatic phrases (e.g., "The house has a life of its own").
     
Examples:
     
       
             
  • That book is mine, not yours. (replaces "my book")
    •          
  • This isn't your jacket; it's hers. (replaces "her jacket")
    •          
  • Our house is smaller than theirs. (replaces "their house")
    •
