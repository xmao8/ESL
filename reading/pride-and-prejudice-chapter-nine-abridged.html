Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Nine (Abridged)

Elizabeth spent the night caring for Jane and was glad to see some improvement in the morning. Still concerned, she sent a message asking her mother to come to Netherfield. Mrs. Bennet soon arrived with Lydia and Kitty. Although she would have been worried if Jane were seriously ill, she was pleased that the illness was not dangerous and secretly hoped Jane would stay longer at Netherfield.

Mr. Bingley welcomed them warmly and showed great concern for Jane’s health. Mrs. Bennet insisted Jane was too ill to be moved, and Bingley quickly agreed. His sisters offered polite attention, though their manners were not as warm. Mrs. Bennet thanked them repeatedly and praised Jane’s gentle nature, while also admiring the beauty of the house and hoping Bingley would remain there for some time.

Elizabeth spoke with Mr. Bingley in a light and playful way, showing her intelligence and wit. He enjoyed the conversation, but Mrs. Bennet warned Elizabeth not to behave so freely in polite company. The discussion soon turned to people and character. Elizabeth suggested that complicated personalities were more interesting, while Mr. Darcy argued that country life offered few opportunities to study a variety of people.

This remark offended Mrs. Bennet, who insisted that there was plenty of society in the neighborhood. Elizabeth tried to explain Darcy’s meaning, but her mother continued to defend the importance of their local social life. To change the subject, Elizabeth asked about Charlotte Lucas, and Mrs. Bennet began speaking about her. She described Charlotte as pleasant but plain, and proudly compared her to Jane, whose beauty she praised enthusiastically.

Mrs. Bennet then told a story about a young man who had once admired Jane and written poetry for her. Elizabeth joked that poetry might actually weaken love rather than strengthen it. Darcy replied that poetry could nourish strong feelings, but Elizabeth argued that it might destroy weaker ones. Their exchange showed their different views but also their growing interest in each other’s ideas.

Mrs. Bennet soon returned to thanking Mr. Bingley for his kindness and prepared to leave. Lydia, however, boldly reminded him of his promise to give a ball. Bingley responded cheerfully that he would keep his promise once Jane recovered. Lydia was delighted and began talking excitedly about future dances and officers.

After Mrs. Bennet and the younger girls left, Elizabeth returned to Jane’s room. Meanwhile, Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst criticized the Bennet family’s behavior. However, Mr. Darcy did not fully agree with them. Although he noticed their faults, he remained impressed by Elizabeth, especially her lively mind and expressive eyes.

Vocabulary List

  • improvement — getting better.
  • concern — worry or care.
  • insisted — strongly said something must happen.
  • admiring — showing approval or liking.
  • wit — clever humor.
  • complicated — not simple.
  • offended — upset or hurt.
  • enthusiastically — with excitement.
  • nourish — to help grow or develop.
  • boldly — in a confident way.
  • promise — something you say you will do.
  • criticized — said negative things.
  • faults — weaknesses or problems.
  • lively — full of energy.
  • expressive — showing feelings clearly.
