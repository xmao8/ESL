The American Revolution: A Fight for Independence

In the mid-18th century, thirteen British colonies stretched along the eastern coast of North America. These colonies were under the rule of Great Britain, but over time, a sense of dissatisfaction began to grow among the colonists. They felt that the British government, located thousands of miles away, was imposing unfair taxes and laws without giving them a voice. This growing tension eventually led to the American Revolution.

One of the main causes of the conflict was "taxation without representation." The British Parliament passed several acts, like the Stamp Act and the Townshend Acts, which placed taxes on goods such as paper, tea, and glass. The colonists argued that since they had no elected representatives in Parliament, Britain had no right to tax them. Protests grew, and groups like the Sons of Liberty organized boycotts of British goods. The Boston Tea Party in 1773, where colonists dumped British tea into the harbor, was a famous act of defiance.

The situation escalated, and in April 1775, the first shots of the war were fired at Lexington and Concord in Massachusetts. This marked the beginning of armed conflict. The colonists, though not a professional army, formed militias and began to organize under leaders like George Washington, who was appointed commander of the Continental Army.

On July 4, 1776, the Continental Congress adopted the Declaration of Independence, a profound document that declared the colonies' separation from Great Britain and stated their belief in fundamental human rights, including "Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness." This act formally established the United States of America as an independent nation.

The war was long and arduous. The Continental Army, often poorly supplied and trained, faced the powerful British military. Key battles, such as the Battle of Saratoga (1777), which convinced France to ally with the Americans, and the harsh winter at Valley Forge, tested the soldiers' resolve. Despite many setbacks, the American forces, aided by French support, continued to fight.

Finally, in 1781, the decisive Battle of Yorktown took place in Virginia. With the help of the French navy, American and French forces trapped the main British army, forcing their surrender. This victory effectively ended the fighting. Two years later, in 1783, the Treaty of Paris was signed, officially recognizing the United States as an independent nation.

The American Revolution was a pivotal event in world history. It established a new nation founded on principles of liberty and self-governance, inspiring other movements for freedom around the globe. It was a testament to the perseverance of ordinary people who dared to challenge a powerful empire for the right to determine their own destiny.

Vocabulary List

Here are important words from the article that can help you understand it better. Try to guess their meaning from the story first, then check the definitions.

  • Dissatisfaction
    Lack of contentment; unhappiness.
  • Imposing
    Forcing (something unwelcome or unfamiliar) to be accepted or put in place.
  • Defiance
    Open resistance; bold disobedience.
  • Escalated
    Increased rapidly in intensity or magnitude.
  • Militias
    A military force that is raised from the civil population to supplement a regular army in an emergency.
  • Profound
    Very great or intense.
  • Arduous
    Involving or requiring strenuous effort; difficult and tiring.
  • Continental Army
    The army of the American colonists during the American Revolutionary War.
  • Alliance
    A union or association formed for mutual benefit, especially between countries or organizations.
  • Resolve
    Firm determination to do something.
  • Decisive
    Producing a definite result; crucial.
  • Surrender
    The act of giving up or yielding to an opponent or demand.
  • Pivotal
    Of crucial importance in relation to the development or success of something else.
  • Perseverance
    Persistence in doing something despite difficulty or delay in achieving success.
  • Empire
    An extensive group of states or countries under a single supreme authority, formerly especially an emperor or empress.
