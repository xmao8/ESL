Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Twenty-Three (Abridged)

Elizabeth was sitting with her family, reflecting on Charlotte's news, when Sir William Lucas appeared to announce his daughter’s engagement.

With many compliments and much self-gratulation on the prospect of a connection between their houses, he unfolded the matter to an audience not merely wondering, but incredulous. Mrs. Bennet protested he must be entirely mistaken, and Lydia, always unguarded and uncivil, boisterously exclaimed—

“Good Lord! Sir William, how can you tell such a story? Do not you know that Mr. Collins wants to marry Lizzy?”

Nothing less than the complaisance of a courtier could have borne such treatment without anger; but Sir William’s good-breeding carried him through. He listened to their impertinence with the most forbearing courtesy.

Elizabeth, feeling it incumbent on her to relieve him from so unpleasant a situation, put herself forward to confirm the account by mentioning her prior knowledge of it from Charlotte herself. She endeavoured to stop the exclamations of her family by the earnestness of her congratulations, in which she was readily joined by Jane, making remarks on the happiness to be expected from the match.

Mrs. Bennet was too overpowered to say much while Sir William remained; but no sooner had he left than her feelings found a rapid vent. She persisted in disbelieving the matter, was sure Mr. Collins had been taken in, and trusted they would never be happy.

Two inferences, however, were plainly deduced from the whole: that Elizabeth was the cause of all the mischief, and that Mrs. Bennet had been barbarously used by them all. Nothing could console and nothing appease her. A week elapsed before she could see Elizabeth without scolding her, a month passed before she could speak to Sir William without being rude, and many months were gone before she could forgive their daughter.

Mr. Bennet’s emotions were much more tranquil. It gratified him to discover that Charlotte Lucas, whom he had used to think tolerably sensible, was as foolish as his wife, and more foolish than his daughter!

Jane confessed herself surprised at the match, but said less of her astonishment than of her earnest desire for their happiness. Kitty and Lydia were far from envying Miss Lucas, for Mr. Collins was only a clergyman; it affected them only as a piece of news to spread at Meryton.

Lady Lucas could not be insensible of triumph on being able to retort on Mrs. Bennet the comfort of having a daughter well married. She called at Longbourn rather oftener than usual, though Mrs. Bennet’s sour looks and ill-natured remarks might have been enough to drive happiness away.

Between Elizabeth and Charlotte there was a restraint which kept them mutually silent. Elizabeth’s disappointment in Charlotte made her turn with fonder regard to her sister Jane, of whose rectitude and delicacy she was sure. She grew daily more anxious for Jane's happiness, as Bingley had now been gone a week and nothing was heard of his return.

Vocabulary List

  • self-gratulation — self-congratulation; pride or satisfaction in one's own actions or fortune.
  • incredulous — unwilling or unable to believe something; skeptical.
  • complaisance — a willingness to please others; compliance or courtesy.
  • forbearing — patient and restrained; demonstrating self-control in the face of provocation.
  • incumbent — necessary for someone as a duty or responsibility.
  • deduced — arrived at a fact or a conclusion by reasoning; inferred.
  • appease — to pacify or placate someone by acceding to their demands; calm.
  • tranquil — free from disturbance; calm, peaceful, and quiet.
  • retort — to say something in answer to a remark or accusation, typically in a sharp, angry, or witty manner.
  • rectitude — morally correct behavior or thinking; righteousness.
