Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Eight

At five o’clock the two ladies retired to dress, and at half-past six Elizabeth was summoned to dinner. To the civil inquiries which then poured in, and among which she had the pleasure of distinguishing the much superior solicitude of Mr. Bingley’s, she could not make a very favorable answer.

Jane was by no means better. The sisters, on hearing this, repeated three or four times how much they were grieved, how shocking it was to have a bad cold, and how excessively they disliked being ill themselves; and then thought no more of the matter.

Their indifference toward Jane, when not immediately before them, restored Elizabeth to the enjoyment of all her former dislike.

She was particularly struck with the difference between Miss Bingley and her brother. He was lively and unreserved, danced every dance, and was angry that the ball closed so early; while his sister was proud and reserved, and thought the assembly beneath her notice.

Mr. Darcy said very little, and Mr. Hurst nothing at all. The former was divided between admiration of the brilliancy which exercise had given to Elizabeth’s complexion, and doubt as to the occasion’s justifying her coming so far alone.

The evening passed pleasantly for Elizabeth. She found herself seated near Mr. Darcy, and though she spoke little, she observed everything. Miss Bingley soon began to talk about balls and society, praising the elegance of London gatherings and criticizing country assemblies.

Elizabeth defended the pleasures of a country ball with quiet humor. She argued that happiness did not depend on fashion, and that people could enjoy themselves just as much without elegance or luxury.

Miss Bingley laughed at this idea and turned the conversation to accomplishments. She declared that a truly accomplished woman must have knowledge of music, singing, drawing, dancing, and modern languages. Darcy added that she must also improve her mind by extensive reading.

Elizabeth was amused and said that she wondered at such high standards, as she had never seen so many accomplished women as they described.

Miss Bingley replied that she knew very few who met these expectations, which made Elizabeth smile.

Later, Mr. Hurst called for cards, and the party divided into players and observers. Elizabeth chose to sit with Jane instead of joining the card game.

Miss Bingley attempted to draw Darcy’s attention by praising his sister and speaking of her accomplishments. Darcy listened politely but did not seem especially interested.

Elizabeth, meanwhile, remained quietly attentive to her sister. Her concern for Jane showed her affection, while her calm behavior contrasted with the artificial manners of the others.

The chapter ends with a growing contrast between Elizabeth’s natural intelligence and the pride and pretension of the Netherfield party.

Vocabulary

  • solicitude — care or concern
  • indifference — lack of interest or concern
  • reserved — quiet and not expressive
  • accomplished — skilled and educated
  • elegance — graceful style or beauty
  • pretension — trying to appear more important than one is
