Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Six (Abridged)

The Bennet sisters soon visited the ladies of Netherfield, and the visit was returned shortly afterward. Jane’s gentle and pleasant manners made a favorable impression on Mr. Bingley’s sisters, though they did not think highly of Mrs. Bennet or the younger girls. Still, they expressed a desire to know Jane and Elizabeth better. Jane was pleased by their attention, but Elizabeth remained doubtful, believing that their politeness came mainly from their brother’s interest in Jane rather than genuine kindness.

It was clear to Elizabeth that Mr. Bingley admired Jane, and she also saw that Jane was beginning to return his feelings. However, Jane’s calm and steady manner prevented others from easily noticing her emotions. Elizabeth admired this quality and believed it protected her sister from gossip or unwanted attention. She shared her thoughts with her friend Charlotte Lucas.

Charlotte, however, did not entirely agree. She believed that Jane’s quiet behavior might actually be a disadvantage. In her opinion, if a woman does not show her feelings clearly, she risks losing the man she cares about. Charlotte argued that affection often needs encouragement to grow and that men may not always recognize a woman’s feelings unless she expresses them openly. She even suggested that women should sometimes show more affection than they truly feel, in order to secure a marriage.

Elizabeth listened but did not accept this idea. She insisted that Jane was not trying to win Mr. Bingley’s affection deliberately and that her feelings were sincere and natural. Jane, she explained, had known Mr. Bingley only a short time and could not yet fully understand her own emotions or his character. Elizabeth believed it was better for feelings to develop honestly rather than through careful planning.

Charlotte, however, maintained her practical view. She believed that happiness in marriage often depends more on chance than on deep understanding. According to her, it was better to marry a suitable man quickly than to wait too long trying to learn everything about him. Elizabeth found this idea amusing but did not think it wise, and she doubted that Charlotte herself would truly follow such advice.

While Elizabeth watched the growing connection between Jane and Mr. Bingley, she did not realize that she herself was beginning to attract Mr. Darcy’s attention. At first, Darcy had not found her particularly beautiful. He had even been critical of her appearance. However, as he observed her more closely, he began to notice the intelligence and liveliness in her expression, especially in her eyes. He also came to appreciate her graceful figure and playful manner of speaking.

Despite these growing feelings, Darcy struggled with his own pride. He tried to convince himself that Elizabeth did not meet the standards of fashionable society, yet he could not ignore the charm of her natural behavior. Elizabeth, however, remained completely unaware of his changing opinion. To her, he was still the proud and disagreeable man who had refused to dance with her at the ball.

Darcy soon found himself wanting to learn more about her. Instead of speaking directly to her, he began by listening carefully to her conversations with others. This behavior did not go unnoticed. During a visit at Sir William Lucas’s house, Elizabeth realized that Darcy was paying attention to her conversation with another gentleman. Curious and slightly amused, she asked Charlotte why he might be doing this. Charlotte replied that only Darcy himself could explain his actions.

Elizabeth jokingly declared that if he continued in this way, she would confront him about it. She recognized that he had a sharp and critical manner, and she felt that the best way to deal with him was to respond with confidence and humor rather than fear. In this way, the chapter shows the beginning of a subtle change in Darcy’s feelings, even though Elizabeth remains unaware of it.

Vocabulary List

  • favorable — positive; showing approval.
  • doubtful — uncertain; not convinced.
  • admire — to like and respect someone.
  • affection — feelings of liking or love.
  • sincere — honest and genuine.
  • practical — focused on real-world results.
  • charm — an attractive quality.
  • graceful — elegant and smooth in movement.
  • pride — a high opinion of oneself.
  • aware — knowing or noticing something.
  • observe — to watch carefully.
  • confidence — belief in oneself.
  • critical — judging or finding faults.
  • confront — to face directly.
  • subtle — small but important.
