Pride and Prejudice — Chapter One

It is a truth widely accepted that a single man with a large fortune must be looking for a wife. When such a man moves into a neighborhood, families quickly become interested, especially those with unmarried daughters.

“My dear Mr. Bennet,” said Mrs. Bennet one day, “have you heard that Netherfield Park has finally been rented?”

Mr. Bennet replied that he had not.

“But it has,” she said excitedly. “Mrs. Long just told me everything. A young man of great fortune from the north of England has taken it. He arrived earlier this week, liked the house immediately, and will move in soon.”

“What is his name?” Mr. Bennet asked.

“Bingley.”

“Is he married or single?”

“Oh, single, of course! A very rich single man. What a wonderful opportunity for our daughters!”

“How does it concern them?” Mr. Bennet asked calmly.

“My dear Mr. Bennet,” she replied, “you must know that I am thinking he may marry one of them.”

Mr. Bennet smiled slightly. “Is that his reason for coming here?”

“Nonsense! But it is very likely he will fall in love with one of them. You must visit him as soon as he arrives.”

“I see no reason to do that,” he said. “You and the girls can visit him, or even better, send them alone. Perhaps he will like you best.”

“You flatter me,” said Mrs. Bennet, “but I am no longer young. A woman with five grown daughters should not think about her own beauty.”

“In such cases, she usually has little beauty left to think about,” Mr. Bennet replied.

“You must go and see Mr. Bingley,” she insisted.

“I am not sure that I will,” he answered.

“But think of your daughters! Sir William and Lady Lucas plan to visit him. They never visit newcomers, so you know how important this is. If you do not go, we cannot meet him properly.”

Mr. Bennet responded playfully, “I am sure Mr. Bingley will be happy to see you. I will send him a letter to give my permission for him to marry whichever daughter he prefers, though I may say a good word for Lizzy.”

“Please do no such thing!” cried Mrs. Bennet. “Lizzy is not better than the others. She is not as beautiful as Jane, nor as cheerful as Lydia. But you always prefer her.”

“None of them are especially remarkable,” Mr. Bennet replied, “but Lizzy is quicker and more intelligent than her sisters.”

“Mr. Bennet, how can you speak that way about your own children? You take pleasure in upsetting me. You have no sympathy for my poor nerves!”

“You misunderstand me,” he said calmly. “I have great respect for your nerves. They have been my companions for many years.”

“You do not know what I suffer,” she said.

“But I hope you will recover,” he replied, “and live to see many more wealthy young men come into the neighborhood.”

“It will not matter if you refuse to visit them,” she answered.

“If twenty come, I will visit them all,” said Mr. Bennet.

Mr. Bennet was a man of sharp intelligence and dry humor, but he was also quiet and unpredictable. Even after many years of marriage, his wife did not fully understand him. Mrs. Bennet, on the other hand, was less complex. She was easily upset, had limited knowledge, and often became nervous when she was unhappy. Her main goal in life was to see her daughters married, and her greatest pleasure was sharing news and visiting others.

Vocabulary List

  • universally — By almost everyone; widely accepted.
  • acknowledged — Accepted or recognized as true.
  • fortune — A large amount of money or wealth.
  • neighbourhood — The area where people live near one another.
  • chaise — A horse-drawn carriage.
  • possession — Control or ownership of something.
  • tiresome — Annoying or boring.
  • scrupulous — Very careful about doing what is proper or correct.
  • vexing — Annoying or upsetting.
  • sarcastic — Using irony to mock or joke.
  • reserve — A quiet, restrained way of behaving.
  • caprice — A sudden change of mood or behavior.
  • temper — A person’s usual emotional state.
  • solace — Comfort in sadness or difficulty.
  • establishment — In this context, a secure marriage and home.
