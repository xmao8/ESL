The Miracle of Dunkirk

In 1940, German soldiers moved quickly through Europe during World War II. Many British and French soldiers were trapped in a small town called Dunkirk in France. They could not escape by land because the German army was too strong. Their only way out was by sea.

There were not enough navy ships to save them. So the British government asked regular people with boats to help. These boats were called “The Little Ships.” They were small fishing boats, sailboats, and even family boats.

From May 26 to June 4, these boats crossed the sea between England and France. It was dangerous. German planes dropped bombs. But the little boats were brave. They helped save over 338,000 soldiers.

People called it the “Miracle of Dunkirk.” It was a surprise that so many men were rescued. The soldiers lost their weapons, but their lives were saved. Later, the British leader Winston Churchill gave a speech. He said, “We shall never surrender.”

This story shows that ordinary people can do great things in hard times. It also shows the power of teamwork and courage.

Vocabulary

Evacuation 🔊
The act of leaving a dangerous place.
Rescue 🔊
To save someone from danger.
Surrender 🔊
To stop fighting and give up.
Channel 🔊
A narrow sea between two pieces of land.
Miracle 🔊
A surprising and lucky event that saves people.

Comprehension Questions

  1. Where were the soldiers trapped?
  2. Why couldn’t the soldiers leave by land?
  3. What kinds of boats helped rescue the soldiers?
  4. How many soldiers were saved?
  5. What message does the story of Dunkirk give us?
