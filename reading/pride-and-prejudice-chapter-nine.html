Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Nine

Elizabeth passed the chief of the night in her sister’s room, and in the morning had the pleasure of being able to send a tolerable answer to the inquiries which she very early received from Mr. Bingley by a housemaid. Though the breakfast parlour was full of ladies, her entrance into it was not attended by any very gracious reception from Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst; their indifference towards Jane, when not immediately before them, restored Elizabeth to the enjoyment of all her former dislike.

Their brother, indeed, was the only one of the party whom she could regard with any complacency. His anxiety for Jane was evident, and his attentions to herself most obliging; and they prevented her feeling herself so much an intruder as she believed she was considered by the others.

She had very little notice from any but him. Miss Bingley was engrossed by Mr. Darcy, her sister scarcely less so; and as for Mr. Hurst, by whom Elizabeth sat, he was an indolent man, who lived only to eat, drink, and play at cards.

When breakfast was over, they were joined by the sisters; and Elizabeth began to like them herself, when she saw how much affection they showed for Jane. They were, in fact, very fine ladies; not deficient in good humour when they were pleased, nor in the power of making themselves agreeable when they chose it; but proud and conceited.

The conversation turned chiefly on the illness of Jane, whose health was a subject of much concern to Mr. Bingley. His sisters declared that they were excessively sorry, and spoke much of their dislike of being ill themselves; but their expressions of sympathy were so general and so little directed to Jane, that Elizabeth could not consider them as sincere.

After breakfast, Mrs. Bennet and her two youngest daughters came to Netherfield. Mrs. Bennet had calculated on seeing Jane better, but was surprised to find her still in bed. She was very much flattered by Mr. Bingley’s attention to her eldest daughter, and listened with pleasure to his expressions of concern.

She soon began to speak of her hopes for Jane’s recovery, and her confidence that she would soon be well again. She also talked of the pleasure she felt in seeing her daughter so well cared for, and of the kindness of the family at Netherfield.

Miss Bingley, however, received Mrs. Bennet with cool politeness, and soon began to criticize the country, comparing it unfavorably with the elegance of London. Mrs. Bennet, though not understanding all her remarks, defended her home with spirit, and declared that she preferred the country to the town.

The conversation then turned to the Bennet family in general. Mrs. Bennet spoke freely of her daughters, praising their beauty and accomplishments. She was particularly proud of Jane, whose beauty she described in glowing terms.

Elizabeth listened with some embarrassment to her mother’s lack of restraint, and could not help feeling that her behavior was improper. Mr. Darcy, who sat near, observed the whole with silent attention, and his opinion of the family was not improved.

Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst listened with smiles of contempt, and occasionally exchanged glances that showed their amusement at Mrs. Bennet’s remarks. Elizabeth felt this keenly, and was ashamed of her mother’s conduct.

Mr. Bingley, however, seemed not to notice anything that could give offense. His attentions were entirely directed toward Jane, and he appeared genuinely pleased with her.

The visit did not last long. Mrs. Bennet, satisfied with what she had seen, soon prepared to leave, taking Lydia and Kitty with her. Elizabeth remained at Netherfield to attend Jane.

As soon as they were gone, Miss Bingley could not help expressing her opinion of the Bennet family. She spoke of them with disdain, and particularly criticized the impropriety of the younger sisters. Mr. Darcy agreed in part, though he admitted that Elizabeth possessed qualities that distinguished her from the rest of her family.

Thus the chapter shows more clearly the differences between the characters: Mr. Bingley’s kindness, his sisters’ pride, Mrs. Bennet’s lack of judgment, and Elizabeth’s growing awareness of her family’s faults.

Vocabulary

  • complacency — a feeling of calm satisfaction
  • indolent — lazy
  • conceited — too proud of oneself
  • sincere — honest and genuine
  • disdain — lack of respect
  • impropriety — bad or improper behavior
