Pride and Prejudice — Chapter Eight (Abridged)

Elizabeth spent the evening at Netherfield caring for her sister Jane, who was still ill with a cold. At dinner, the others asked politely about Jane’s health, though Elizabeth noticed that their concern quickly faded when Jane was not present. Only Mr. Bingley showed genuine interest and kindness. His sisters expressed sympathy, but their attention soon turned elsewhere.

This behavior strengthened Elizabeth’s dislike of Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst. She saw clearly that their kindness toward Jane came mainly from their brother’s admiration rather than from true affection. In contrast, Mr. Bingley remained warm, friendly, and attentive to everyone.

The evening passed with conversation among the guests. Miss Bingley began to compare the pleasures of city life with those of the country, praising the elegance of London society and suggesting that country gatherings were less refined. Elizabeth disagreed politely, arguing that people could enjoy themselves just as much in the country. She believed that happiness depended more on people’s attitudes than on wealth or fashion.

Mr. Darcy listened quietly, occasionally joining the conversation. Though he did not speak much, he paid close attention to Elizabeth. He was struck by her intelligence, her lively expressions, and the brightness of her eyes, which seemed even more noticeable after her walk through the muddy fields. At the same time, he questioned whether such behavior—walking alone across the countryside—was proper for a young lady.

Miss Bingley, however, was less interested in Elizabeth’s ideas and more concerned with attracting Mr. Darcy’s attention. She often directed the conversation toward topics she thought would please him. Soon, the discussion turned to the idea of an “accomplished woman.”

Miss Bingley declared that a truly accomplished woman must have many talents, including skill in music, singing, drawing, dancing, and languages. She also needed to have elegant manners and a fashionable appearance. Mr. Darcy added that she must also improve her mind through extensive reading.

Elizabeth listened with amusement and expressed doubt. She said she was surprised that so many accomplished women existed, as she had rarely met anyone who met such high expectations. Miss Bingley responded that she knew very few women who truly deserved the title. This exchange revealed Elizabeth’s practical thinking and her willingness to question unrealistic standards.

As the evening continued, Mr. Hurst suggested playing cards, and some of the group joined him. Elizabeth chose not to participate and instead stayed with Jane, showing her strong sense of loyalty and care. Meanwhile, Miss Bingley continued her efforts to engage Mr. Darcy, praising his sister and discussing her accomplishments. Although Darcy listened politely, he did not respond with much enthusiasm.

Elizabeth’s quiet concern for her sister stood in contrast to the artificial manners of the others. She remained sincere and natural, while Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst seemed more focused on appearances and social advantage.

Throughout the evening, Darcy found himself increasingly interested in Elizabeth, though he did not openly admit it. He admired her intelligence and independence, even while struggling with his own pride and expectations. Elizabeth, however, remained unaware of his growing admiration. To her, he was still the proud man who had once insulted her.

This chapter highlights the differences between characters: Bingley’s kindness, Darcy’s reserved nature, Elizabeth’s intelligence, and the Bingley sisters’ pride. It also deepens the developing relationships, especially the contrast between genuine feeling and social ambition.

Vocabulary

  • genuine — real and sincere
  • refined — elegant and cultured
  • accomplished — skilled and educated
  • expectation — a belief about what should happen
  • loyalty — strong support for someone
  • admiration — strong liking or respect
